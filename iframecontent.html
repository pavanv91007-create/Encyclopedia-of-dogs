Dogs (Canis familiaris)

Dogs are domesticated descendants of wolves, often called "man's best friend" and known for their loyalty, high intelligence, and diverse breeds. They were the first species to be domesticated over 14,000 years ago, with strong senses of smell and hearing.

Breeds

There are around 450 official dog breeds, ranging from the tiny Chihuahua to the massive Great Dane.

Sensory Abilities

Dogs have 125 million to 300 million smell-sensitive receptors (compared to 6 million in humans). Their hearing is about four times more sensitive than humans.

Communication

They use barks, whines, growls, and body language (tail wagging, ear position) to communicate.

Diet

As domesticated carnivores, they can thrive on a starch-rich diet.

Unique Features

Each dog has a unique nose print, similar to human fingerprints. They also have a third eyelid called a "haw" for eye protection and lubrication.

Roles

Dogs serve roles including companionship, protection, therapy, and assisting police and the disabled.

Lifespan

The average lifespan is 10 to 13 years, with smaller breeds often living longer.

Health and Care

Toxic Foods: Chocolate, grapes, raisins, and onions are harmful and can be fatal to dogs.

Cooling Down: Dogs pant and sweat through their paw pads to cool down.

Training: Dogs can understand roughly 100 to 250 words and gestures.

Common Behaviors

"3-3-3" Rule: A guide for new adoptions—3 days to decompress, 3 weeks to learn routine, 3 months to feel at home.

Contagious Yawning: Dogs may yawn when they see or hear a human yawn, potentially signalling empathy.

Tail Wagging: A wag to the right often indicates happiness, while a wag to the left can mean fear.

Circling: Many dogs circle before lying down, a habit inherited from ancestors who flattened grass for comfort.

Nose Wetness: A wet nose helps absorb scent chemicals.
